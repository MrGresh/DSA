## 🛠 Useful Patterns to Remember

### 1. Max-Heap vs. Min-Heap for K-Selection
* To find the **Kth Largest**: Use a **Min-Heap** of size $K$. After processing all elements, the root is your answer.
* To find the **Kth Smallest**: Use a **Max-Heap** of size $K$. 
* **Rule of Thumb:** If the problem says "Largest/Frequent," you usually need a Min-Heap to keep the "best" $K$ and discard the smaller ones.

### 2. The Two-Heaps Pattern
Essential for maintaining a dynamic median.
* **Left Side:** Max-Heap (stores smaller half of elements).
* **Right Side:** Min-Heap (stores larger half of elements).
* Keep the size difference between the two heaps $\le 1$.

### 3. Merging Multi-way Sorted Data
When merging $K$ sorted arrays/lists, push the first element of each into a Min-Heap. When you pop an element, push the next element from the **same list** that the popped element came from.


### 4. Greedy Selection with Heap
In problems like "Connect Ropes" or "Huffman Coding," the goal is to always pick the two smallest elements, combine them, and put them back. A Min-Heap handles this in $O(\log n)$.

### 5. Heap as a Frequency Tracker
Combined with a Hashmap, Heaps are perfect for "Top K Frequent" problems. The map tracks counts, and the Heap keeps the top $K$ keys based on those counts.

### 6. Dijkstra/Prim (Priority Queue)
In graph theory, a Min-Heap is used to always extract the node with the minimum distance (or minimum edge weight) to explore next, reducing time from $O(V^2)$ to $O(E \log V)$.
