## 🛠 Useful Patterns to Remember

### 1. Sorting is Key
Most Greedy problems require sorting the input first.
* **By End Time:** For interval/activity selection (pick the one that finishes earliest).
* **By Value/Weight Ratio:** For Fractional Knapsack.
* **By Profit:** For Job Sequencing.

### 2. The Local vs. Global Choice
A Greedy algorithm works only if the problem has the **Greedy Choice Property** (a global optimum can be reached by choosing a local optimum) and **Optimal Substructure**.

### 3. Greedy vs. Dynamic Programming
If you cannot make a choice without looking at future consequences (like the 0/1 Knapsack problem), Greedy will fail and you must use **DP**.


### 4. Priority Queues (Heaps)
In many medium/hard greedy problems (like Huffman Coding or Connect Ropes), a **Min-Heap** is used to repeatedly pick the two smallest elements efficiently in $O(\log n)$.

### 5. Interval Scheduling
When dealing with "meeting rooms" or "platforms," sorting by **Start Time** and using a Min-Heap to track the earliest **End Time** of currently occupied rooms is a standard approach.

### 6. Graph Greedy
Algorithms like Prim’s, Kruskal’s, and Dijkstra’s are "Greedy" because they always pick the "cheapest" edge or "closest" vertex available at the current moment.
